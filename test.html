Introduction

Japan has seen a significant rise in non-regular employment due to the 2018 work style reform (Hatarakikata Kaiku) and economic fluctuations. This study by Mariko Enoue compares mental health outcomes among non-regular, regular, and gig workers in terms of stress levels. The findings have relevant policy implications for achieving Sustainable Development Goal 8.8: Protect labor rights and promote safe and secure working environments for all workers, including those in precarious employment.

Employment Status Categories

  • Regular workers (full-time employees with lifelong contracts)
  • Non-regular workers (part-time workers, temporary agency workers, and contract workers)
  • Self-employed individuals
  • Gig workers ("crowdwork" and "work on-demand via apps" for short-term or one-time tasks)

Key Findings

Results and Policy Implications

  • Regular workers with gig work experience had the highest odds ratio of psychological distress, followed by non-regular workers with gig work experience.
  • Men engaging in gig work showed higher levels of psychological distress than those not participating in gig work within the same employment category.
  • Self-employed individuals exclusively engaged in gig work had lower psychological distress than expected.
  • The rise of gig-work in Japan does not fit well with the current occupational health system and adds pressure on individuals, potentially widening health disparities.
  • It is essential to reassess health policies, particularly those concerning side gig work for workers, regardless of their employment status, to safeguard everyone's health and working conditions.
