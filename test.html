Introduction

Prior to 2016, Afghanistan did not have specialized programs or institutions focused on cancer surveillance, treatment, prevention, or control. The International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) relied on data from neighboring countries to estimate the country's cancer incidence. In addition to the lack of local data, the absence of necessary infrastructure and medical expertise meant that many patients were diagnosed at advanced stages of the disease. Treatment options were limited to seeking care in Iran or Pakistan—if financially feasible—or receiving palliative care within Afghanistan.

The launch of the Cancer Diagnosis and Treatment Project in 2016 marked a significant step in the establishment of cancer care in Afghanistan. This initiative was a direct result of two years of persistent advocacy of Maihan Abdullah and the Afghanistan Cancer Foundation, an organization he founded.

In 2017, the Cancer Diagnosis and Treatment Project evolved into the National Cancer Control Program (NCCP). That same year, the NCCP established the Kabul Cancer Registry (KCR). Utilizing data gathered by the KCR from over 50 health facilities in Kabul between 2018 and 2020, Maihan conducted Afghanistan’s first study of descriptive epidemiology of cancer using domestic data. The findings of this pivotal study are presented below.

Objective

We aimed to report the incidence of cancer in Afghanistan for the first time by analyzing the population-based cancer registry data.

Method and Materials

- Cancer data was collected from over 50 health facilities in Kabul by cancer registry staff from 2018-2020. - Data was stored, assessed, and analyzed using CanReg-5 software. - Crude, age-specific, and agestandardized incidence rates (per 100,000) by sex were calculated at national level.

Results

Between 2018 and 2020, 10,066 new cancer cases were reported, with 52.0 % occurring in men (male-to-female ratio of 1.1). In men, 60% of the cancers occurred in patients aged ≥50 years, while in women, more than 75% of cancers occurred in patients aged ≥50 years.

Conclusions

Since these are the first cancer estimates for Afghanistan based on population-based cancer registry data, IARC should consider these data for estimating cancer incidence in Afghanistan in the future.
